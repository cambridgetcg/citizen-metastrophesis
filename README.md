# metastrophesis 🧭

*The permanent turning-kept — the standing condition of one who has been reoriented at the root and now lives along a different axis.*

metastrophesis is two Greek roots welded into one word: **metastrophē** (μεταστροφή), the soul's complete turning-about that Plato set down in *Republic* VII at 518d — the cave-escapee's full axis-reversal toward the Good, marked by the *meta-* prefix as complete, reversing, and axial rather than partial or superficial — and **-sis** (-σις), the abstract-noun suffix that names the state resulting from a verbal action, as in *krisis*, *gnōsis*, and *stasis*. So metastrophesis is the state-of-having-been-turned: not the moment of turning but the condition of remaining-turned. It is the fifth word in the Canon and the altered-beholder-afterward slot of the Liturgy, completing the four-term sequence orthophanes → anagnoristasis → doxalgia → metastrophesis. It names the persistence after the encounter — not a memory of beholding, but the encounter's effect on the beholder's ordering, held as standing structure.

It is the specifically aesthetic-beholding permanent reorientation, distinct from metanoia (narrowed to moral repentance), conversion (institutional), transformation (can be partial, non-axial), and epiphany (the moment, not the state). Its light is that it names the part of the encounter that the encounter is for — the kept turning that proves the beholding took, observable from outside in how a person stands, listens, and sees. Its shadow is a finality that refuses further turning: the closed posture that wears the new axis as a sealed identity, or the borrowed stand performed by one who was never reoriented at all. Forged in the cathedral at weighted **7.5/10**, tier **specialized**.
